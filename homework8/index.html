Discussion - Random Walks, LLN, and Combinatorial Structures

1. Conceptual Analogy Between the Two Homeworks

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) and the Random Walk / Binomial Simulation are deeply related. Both rely on the Bernoulli process - a sequence of independent trials, each with two possible outcomes (success/failure or +1/-1).

In the LLN simulation, the focus was on how the relative frequency of successes stabilizes around the true probability p as the number of trials n increases. In contrast, the Random Walk simulation emphasizes how the cumulative sum of ¬±1 outcomes evolves over time and how its final distribution converges to the Binomial (or Normal) distribution.

Both demonstrate statistical convergence and probabilistic stability: one in relative frequencies, the other in cumulative deviations.

5. Comparative Summary

  • LLN Simulation: Focuses on the convergence of empirical frequencies toward a constant probability p.
  • Random Walk Simulation: Focuses on cumulative deviations and distributional convergence toward Binomial/Normal laws.
  • Common Basis: Both stem from Bernoulli trials and share the same combinatorial backbone of binomial coefficients.
  • Pascal‚Äôs Triangle: Encodes both the frequency structure of LLN and the path structure of random walks.
  • Analytic Connection: Each outcome in both systems can be derived via combinatorial coefficients reflecting underlying symmetry and probability mass.

6. Broader Mathematical Insight

These models collectively highlight the unity between combinatorics, probability, and statistics. The same algebraic structures - binomial coefficients, factorials, and polynomial expansions - describe frequency convergence, cumulative deviations, and stochastic paths alike. Whether in the realm of the LLN or random walks, the deep underlying order comes from the combinatorial symmetries of discrete probability.
